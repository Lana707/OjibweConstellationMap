Please note there are mulitple interpretations and versions of these stories, many being passed through oral tradition rather than documented, those listed are the most widely represented online, all sources are provided.

Ojiig - Fisher

There are many variations of this story, some say Fisher was inspired by Bear to bring warmth, others state it was Fisher’s son. Generally speaking most stories about Fisher were that he wanted to bring warmth to an everlasting winter and that he freed birds from the sky world. Many versions also say that he was shot by an arrow.

Below is one variation of the tale.

Fisher has grown tired of the eternal winter, limiting his and his son’s abilities to hunt. His son was told by a squirrel that he must cry to his father, the Fisher, about the cold, so he did. Upon hearing this, Fisher was determined to bring warmth. Fisher invited his friends, Otter, Lynx and Wolverine to a feast where he told them about his plan to the skyland where there is warmth and bring some back to their home. His friends agreed to help and followed him on his journey. As they approached the top of the highest mountain, they need to jump high enough to break through the land above them. Otter, Lynx and Fisher attempted but failed. Wolverine was the strongest of them and was determined to break through. He jumped and failed but preserved until he was able to create a crack, he jumped again creating a hole which Fisher then jumped through. The skyland was lush, warm and full of life. Fisher noticed birds trapped in cages and wanted to free them for hunting, so he did. The sky people noticed Fisher and his friends and began chasing them and calling them thieves. Wolverine left through the hole but Fisher continued to chew to make the hole bigger so that it could not be closed. The size of the hole was big enough to bring warmth for about half of the year. Fisher taunted the sky people so they would chase him and he climbed into a tree where they began shooting him with arrows. Fisher could only be killed by a specific spot on his tail, but the sky people were able to hit him there. The spirits decided to put Fisher among the stars, during the winter Fisher is on his back, but once winter is about to end he is back on his feet chasing the warmth.

Maang - Loon

The clan system in Ojibwe culture consists of seven clans. Those belonging to the Crane Clan and Loon Clan are considered natural leaders. To reflect the importance of the Loon Clan and their leadership, the constellation containing the seven brightest stars is named after the loon. The loon’s black back with white dots is also perceived as representing the night sky.

Mooz - Moose

The Mooz constellation appears in the fall and some believe that it represents the strength of the moose. As the constellation moves west with the winter season, the moose begin to lose their antlers and become weaker. Before hunting a moose or anything, it is tradition to offer Asemaa. After killing a moose, many Ojibwe communities believe in hanging the moose beard from a tree to respect and honour the Mooz constellation sky.

Nanaboujou

Nanaboujou is also known as Nanabush, Nanabijou, Nanaboozhoo and more.

Nanaboujou is a prominent figure in many Ojibwe stories, he is known not only for being a trickster but as a cultural shaping figure. He is also a central figure in the creation story. In the constellation he is believed to be hunting Curly Tail as his arrow is pointed towards it. Some varying interpretations of the constellation include sash or tail from frog skin at the end.

Mishi Bizhiw - Curly Tail

The Curly Tail constellation represents the Great Underwater Panther and is the main spring constellation. When the constellation is visible, Ojibwe communities relocate to their spring camps where many make maple sugar. It also represented that the ice would be melting and that it was dangerous to travel on water. Before travelling by canoe some would offer tobacco to travel safely.

Madoodiswan - Sweat Lodge

The Sweat Lodge constellation pairs with the Noondeshin Bemaadizid (Exhausted Bather) constellation. Different cultures have varying customs for Sweat Lodge rituals however the general shape typically remains the same with a wooden pole frame and with animal skin or different materials to enclose the space. Rocks heated by fire are usually faced east and these stones are referred to as Grandfathers. The Grandfathers remain in the center of the sweat lodge and placed in a hole where sometimes water is added to increase the amount of steam. During this time prayer and purification may occur. The sweat lodge ritual serves as a reminder of origins and considered a healing process.

Reference

Noondeshin Bemaadizid - Exhausted Bather

The Exhausted Bather is a constellation that pairs with the Sweat Lodge constellation. It depicts a person who is exhausted from completing the Sweat Lodge ceremony. Some of these ceremonies can last over an hour using multiple Grandfathers which are heated rocks and be quite tiring.

Reference

Madoodoowasiniig - Stones of the Sweat Lodge

Madoodoowasiniig is not a constellation connected by lines but this star cluster is believed to represent Grandfathers which are the heated stones used in Sweat Lodge rituals.

Reference

Bugonagiizhig - Hole in Sky

This constellation is related to some Ojibwe creation stories where humans were first dropped onto earth through that hole. Some also believe that it is connected to the shaking tent ceremony as a spiritual doorway from the tent built by a medicine person.

Ajijaak - Crane/Skeleton Bird

The clan system in Ojibwe culture consists of seven clans. Those belonging to the Crane Clan and Loon Clan are considered natural leaders. The crane constellation usually appears in summertime for a couple hours after sunset.

Reference

Biboonkeonini - Winter-Maker

Winter-maker is the primary constellation seen during winter months. Some believed that you could only tell stories during winter when there is snow, otherwise spirits will come to play tricks.

Reference

Aadawaa’amoog/Odaadawaa’amoog

The three stars in the middle of Winter-Maker are referred to as Aadawaa’amoog or Odaadawaa’amoog. These stars are believed to depict three deceased spirits travelling to Jiibay Ziibi (River of Souls).
